Tropical weather forecasts are different from those at higher latitudes. The sun hits the tropics more directly at high latitudes (at least on average over a year), causing the tropics to warm (Stevens 2011). And, the vertical direction (up, when a person stands on the surface of the Earth) is perpendicular to the axis of rotation of the Earth at the equator, while the axis of rotation and the vertical are the same at the poles; this makes the Earth's rotation affect atmospheric circulation more strongly at higher-lower latitudes. Because of these two factors, clouds and rainstorms in the tropics can occur more naturally than those at higher latitudes, where they are more tightly controlled by larger-scale forces. in the atmosphere. Because of these differences, clouds and rain are more difficult to forecast in the tropics than at high latitudes. Temperature, on the other hand, is easy to forecast in the tropics, since it doesn't vary much.
